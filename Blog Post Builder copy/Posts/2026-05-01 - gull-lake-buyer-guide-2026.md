# Buying a Home on Gull Lake: A 2026 Guide

If you're shopping for a place on Gull Lake, you're already in the busiest lake-home market in Minnesota north of the Twin Cities. That's not hype — it's volume. Nearly 10,000 acres of water, four towns sharing the shoreline, and the Brainerd Lakes Area's biggest concentration of resorts, golf, and full-time lake homes. This is the working buyer's guide for 2026: the basins, the towns, the price bands, the fishing, the inspection list, and the parts of the lake that don't show up in a listing photo.

## The lake itself: bigger than people expect, shallower than they think

Gull Lake is 9,947 acres, give or take a few — the largest lake inside East Gull Lake city limits and one of the largest in the broader Brainerd Lakes region. It runs about 15.3 miles north to south and 3.3 miles wide at its widest. Maximum depth is 80 feet. But here's the part that matters when you're looking at a specific lot: nearly 30% of the lake is 15 feet or less, with a sand-and-gravel bottom across most of the shallows and median depth around 30 feet. Translation — there's plenty of deep water, but most of the shoreline you'll actually own sits over relatively shallow, swimmable, sandy bottom. That's a feature, not a bug, but it changes how you think about dock placement and depth.

The lake is connected by a narrow channel to Upper Gull Lake to the north, and it drains south through the Gull River to a federally managed [Gull Lake Reservoir Dam](https://www.mnhs.org/mnopedia/gull-lake-dam) built in 1912 — one of the six Mississippi headwaters reservoirs. Lake levels are regulated by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, which means you don't get the wild seasonal swings some unregulated lakes deal with. Predictable water levels are quietly one of the best things about owning here.

Plenty of buyers don't realize Gull is split between two counties. The Cass County side covers most of the northern and western shoreline (and includes most of East Gull Lake and Lake Shore). The Crow Wing County side wraps the southern shoreline into Brainerd. Property tax rates differ between the counties, and that's worth a 30-second conversation with your agent before you submit an offer — it's not a deal-breaker, but it's a real number.

## The four towns on the shore — and why it matters which one

Most Minnesota lakes have one town on them, if any. Gull Lake has four, and they have genuinely different characters.

**Nisswa** sits north of the lake, about a mile inland. It's the social capital — the walkable downtown, the Friday-night turtle races in the summer, the boutiques and breweries. If you want a lake home where "going into town" means walking somewhere with a sidewalk, Nisswa-side property is the play.

**East Gull Lake** is the actual city wrapping the western shore. It's quieter, more residential, more lake-home than lake-town. Cragun's, Madden's, and Grand View Lodge are all on this side, which means you've got world-class golf and lakeshore restaurants minutes from anywhere on the western shoreline.

**Lake Shore** holds the northern shoreline along Highway 77. It's where you'll find some of the older, established cabin neighborhoods plus newer high-end builds. More wooded, slightly less density.

**Brainerd** itself touches the southern end. This is the in-town side — closer to the airport (Brainerd Lakes Regional, plus options out of Minneapolis-St. Paul under three hours away), closer to the hospital, closer to the year-round services. Brainerd-side Gull Lake homes are often the practical choice for full-time residents.

You're not picking a town when you buy on Gull Lake. You're picking a stretch of shoreline that happens to be in one. But the town determines your commute to groceries, your school district, your tax rate, and the character of your neighborhood. Worth thinking about up front.

## What you're actually buying: four broad property types

Sellers and listing agents tend to lump everything together as "lakefront." It's more useful to break it down:

- **Original mid-century cabins** — built in the 1950s through the 1970s, typically on smaller lots with shorter shoreline frontage. Often teardown candidates if the lot itself is good. Range starts in the low-to-mid six figures.
- **Updated cabins** — same bones, with one or two renovations and a working kitchen, septic, and roof. The middle of the market.
- **Year-round lake homes** — modern construction, full insulation, lake-side living rooms, double garages. Most of the active inventory in 2026 falls here.
- **Resort-adjacent properties and luxury estates** — the upper end. Long shoreline, often a guest house, sometimes shared dock easements with neighboring properties. Big Gull Lake holds a meaningful number of these on the western shore near the resort corridor.

A common mistake first-time Gull Lake buyers make is shopping for "a cabin" and then realizing six months in that they wanted a year-round home. If you can imagine yourself ever using the place in February — for ice fishing, for snowmobiling, for a long weekend — buy something that's actually winterized. The cost difference closes fast over a few years of ownership.

## The fishing reputation, with the actual numbers

Gull Lake's fishing reputation is real, and unusually for a Minnesota lake, it's heavily managed. The Minnesota DNR has stocked at least 2.8 million walleye fry into Gull Lake since 2003, with continued stocking of roughly one to two million fry annually over the past decade. That's a lot of fish. The trade-off is that Gull is more of a "good fishing for a lot of anglers" lake than a "trophy walleye" destination — average walleye in recent surveys ran around 14.6 inches and 1.3 pounds. You're not winning the Governor's Fishing Opener here, but you're catching dinner.

Where Gull genuinely shines is the multi-species story. Anglers commonly target largemouth bass, smallmouth bass, walleye, panfish (bluegill and crappie), muskie, northern pike, yellow perch, burbot (eelpout), and even common carp on the right day. Smallmouth fishing in the rocky shallows on the east side is some of the better bass water in the Brainerd region.

Before you submit an offer on a specific lot, pull the [DNR LakeFinder survey for Gull Lake](https://www.dnr.state.mn.us/lakefind/index.html) and look at the most recent fish survey, water clarity readings, and the maps showing depth contours. You'll often find that a lot you assumed was great fishing-frontage is actually over flat sand with no structure — and the lot two doors down has a rock pile 40 feet from shore. That kind of thing matters.

For broader lake stewardship, the [Gull Chain of Lakes Association](https://gcola.org/) is the organized homeowner group that tracks water quality, fights aquatic invasives, and advocates on shoreline issues. A good Gull Lake agent will know who's involved.

## The 2026 price story

Here's where Gull Lake gets expensive. As of April 2026, the average listing price across Gull Lake-area properties is sitting around $1.5M, with active waterfront listings ranging from roughly $145,000 at the low end to $2.85M at the upper end [verify with current MLS]. East Gull Lake homes with views are running a median list of $2.5M [verify with current MLS]. The Brainerd Lakes Area broadly shows around 75 active lake property listings averaging just under $600K, but Gull Lake specifically skews well above the area average — it's the premium lake in the region.

Days on market across the Brainerd Lakes Area run roughly 80 days in 2026, with waterfront inventory in Q1 2026 down about 21% from the prior year. Inventory is tighter, prices are holding, and the market is generally favorable to buyers compared to 2021–2022 — but Gull Lake's best lots still see fast offers when they hit MLS in the spring window.

What moves price most on Gull Lake, in rough order:

1. **Linear feet of frontage.** More than total lot size. A 100-foot lot with a great shoreline beats a 200-foot lot with a swamp.
2. **Bottom and depth at the dock.** Sandy, swimmable shallow with a quick drop to four-plus feet at the lift is the sweet spot.
3. **Orientation.** West-facing lots get the long summer evenings. East-facing get the morning sun and protected wind. Both are valuable; pick what you'll actually use.
4. **Privacy and tree cover.** Mature pines on a lot are nearly impossible to replace.
5. **Distance to a town and to a marina.** Closer is more expensive, but distance also means quieter water.
6. **Year-round buildable.** Septic, well, road access in February. The premium for genuinely year-round property is real.

## The inventory cycle: what May 2026 actually looks like

Listings on Gull Lake peak in late April through June. That's right now. Sellers list ahead of summer showings; buyers come up from the Cities for Memorial Day weekend and write offers within days of their first walk-through. Best lakefront stock typically clears by mid-July. By late August what's left is either overpriced, has a known flaw, or is a seller who waited too long. October through March sees thinner inventory but real negotiating room — patient buyers do well in the off-season.

If you're shopping right now, two practical moves: get on a listings alert today (the best lots don't make it to Zillow's Saturday refresh), and have your inspector and lender lined up before you see anything. Gull Lake offers move fast.

## A short checklist before you sign

The things that catch first-time lake-home buyers off guard:

- **Septic.** Conforming, non-conforming, mound system, drain field condition. A failing septic on a lakefront lot is a five-figure problem at minimum.
- **Shoreline classification.** Minnesota lakeshore zoning regulates setbacks, riprap, vegetation, and dock placement. Verify the lot complies and that anything you want to build will be approvable.
- **Dock and lift permits.** Some bays have restrictions. Confirm the dock you're seeing is actually permitted.
- **Well water testing.** Standard, but skipped surprisingly often.
- **Easements.** Shared lake access easements, road easements, dock easements. Read them before you fall in love.
- **Lake level history.** Predictable on Gull thanks to the dam, but ask anyway.

## Who Gull Lake is right for

Gull Lake is right for the buyer who wants real lake life in the most-developed lake market in northern Minnesota. People who want golf and fine dining and resort amenities within five minutes of the dock. Families who'll use the place enough that proximity to Nisswa, healthcare, and a real airport matters. Buyers who want a deep market with reliable resale, not a one-off lot on an obscure lake.

Gull Lake is probably not right for the buyer who wants 80 quiet acres of nobody. The shoreline here is developed, the summer water is busy, and the price floor is high. If that's the priority, look at smaller waters in [Crow Wing County](/lake/round-lake/) or further north on the Whitefish Chain — quieter, cheaper per foot of frontage, less amenity.
